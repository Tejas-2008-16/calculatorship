Taxation on Mutual Funds in India: LTCG, STCG, and How to Minimise Your Tax Bill

Mutual fund returns look great on paper — until taxes arrive. India's post-Budget 2024 tax framework has changed significantly, with new LTCG rates, a revised debt fund regime and updated exemption limits. Here's a definitive guide to exactly how much you owe and, more importantly, how to legally owe less.

Understanding how your mutual fund gains are taxed is not optional — it's essential. Tax drag can reduce your effective CAGR by 1–3% annually, which over 20 years can translate to lakhs of rupees in foregone wealth. The good news: with the right structure, you can legally minimise this drag significantly.

Equity Fund Taxation

Equity mutual funds are defined by SEBI as funds investing at least 65% of their corpus in Indian equities. This category includes large-cap, mid-cap, small-cap, multi-cap, ELSS, flexi-cap, sectoral and thematic funds.

Long-Term Capital Gains (LTCG) — Held more than 12 months:

As per the Finance Act 2024 (effective 23 July 2024), LTCG on equity mutual funds is taxed at 12.5% (without indexation benefit). The first ₹1.25 lakh of LTCG per financial year is completely exempt from tax. This exemption limit was raised from ₹1 lakh to ₹1.25 lakh in Budget 2024.

LTCG Tax Calculation — Equity Funds
Taxable LTCG = Total LTCG – ₹1,25,000 exemption
Tax Payable = Taxable LTCG × 12.5%

Example: You earned ₹2,00,000 in LTCG this year
Taxable amount = ₹2,00,000 – ₹1,25,000 = ₹75,000
Tax = ₹75,000 × 12.5% = ₹9,375

Short-Term Capital Gains (STCG) — Held less than 12 months:

If you redeem equity fund units within 12 months of purchase, all gains are classified as STCG and taxed at a flat rate of 20% (raised from 15% by the Finance Act 2024). There is no basic exemption on STCG — the full gain is taxed at 20% regardless of your income tax slab.

Smart Strategy: Harvesting your LTCG up to ₹1.25 lakh each financial year is entirely legal and tax-free. If your total equity LTCG for the year is under ₹1.25L, you owe zero tax on it. Investors with large equity portfolios can strategically book and reinvest this amount annually to reset their cost basis and avoid a large taxable event later.

Debt Fund Taxation

This is where the most significant recent change occurred. Prior to 1 April 2023, debt mutual funds enjoyed the indexation benefit — long-term gains (held over 3 years) were taxed at 20% after adjusting the purchase price for inflation (using the Cost Inflation Index). This made debt funds highly tax-efficient for investors in the 30% tax bracket.

Critical Change — April 2023: The Finance Act 2023 abolished indexation benefit for debt funds entirely. From 1 April 2023 onwards, ALL gains from debt mutual funds — regardless of holding period — are added to your total income and taxed at your applicable income tax slab rate. There is no separate LTCG rate or exemption for debt funds. A 30% slab taxpayer now pays 30% on all debt fund gains, making debt funds significantly less tax-efficient than before.

This change applies to funds where equity allocation is less than 65%, covering: pure debt funds, overnight funds, liquid funds, ultra-short duration funds, money market funds, dynamic bond funds, corporate bond funds, gilt funds, and hybrid funds with equity below 65%.

ELSS Taxation

Equity Linked Savings Schemes (ELSS) are a unique category of equity mutual funds that offer a tax deduction under Section 80C of the Income Tax Act. They come with a mandatory 3-year lock-in — the shortest lock-in among all 80C instruments.

ELSS & Section 80C: ELSS is the only mutual fund category eligible for deduction under Section 80C. Investments up to ₹1.5 lakh per financial year can be deducted from your taxable income, potentially saving ₹46,800 in tax (at the 30% slab + 4% cess). This makes ELSS a double-benefit instrument: tax savings on entry + equity growth over time.

Upon redemption after the mandatory 3-year lock-in, gains are classified as LTCG and taxed at 12.5% with the ₹1.25L exemption — the same as any other equity fund. However, since each SIP instalment has its own 3-year lock-in from the date of that instalment, systematic investors need to track each unit's purchase date carefully. The first instalment unlocks in year 3, the second in year 4, and so on.

Despite the 12.5% LTCG tax on exit, ELSS remains highly attractive because the 30% tax saved on entry (for investors in the highest slab) far outweighs the 12.5% paid on exit — especially when gains compound over many years.

Dividend Tax

Prior to April 2020, mutual fund dividends were tax-free in the hands of investors because funds paid Dividend Distribution Tax (DDT) at the source. This changed with the Finance Act 2020, which abolished DDT entirely.

From FY 2020-21 onwards, mutual fund dividends are added to the investor's gross income and taxed at their applicable income tax slab rate — exactly like salary or FD interest. Additionally, if an investor's dividend income from a single mutual fund AMC exceeds ₹5,000 in a financial year, the AMC is required to deduct TDS at 10% before crediting the dividend.

For most long-term investors in the 20–30% tax bracket, this makes the growth (reinvestment) option far more tax-efficient than the dividend option. The dividend option is really only relevant for retirees in the 0% or 5% slab who need regular income and have no better source.

Tax Harvesting Strategy

Tax harvesting is the practice of strategically booking gains or losses to minimise your overall tax liability. It is perfectly legal and widely used by sophisticated investors. Here's how it works in the Indian context:

LTCG Harvesting (Profit Booking):

  • Each financial year, the first ₹1.25 lakh of equity LTCG is completely tax-free
  • If your long-held equity funds have unrealised gains, consider redeeming enough units each March to book ₹1.25L in LTCG
  • Immediately reinvest the proceeds in the same fund — your cost basis resets to the current (higher) NAV
  • You've legally crystallised ₹1.25L in tax-free gains and reduced your future tax burden
  • Do this annually from year 2 or 3 of your SIP journey and you could save ₹15,000–₹20,000 per year in future taxes

Loss Harvesting (Tax-Loss Booking):

  • If a fund is in loss, you can redeem and book the capital loss
  • Short-term capital losses can be set off against both STCG and LTCG
  • Long-term capital losses can only be set off against LTCG
  • Unabsorbed losses can be carried forward for 8 assessment years
Key Strategy: Combining annual LTCG harvesting (booking ₹1.25L tax-free each year) with systematic SIP investing can meaningfully reduce your effective tax rate over a long investment horizon. This is one of the most powerful and underused tax planning tools available to Indian equity investors — no special CA required.

Key Takeaways

  • Hold equity funds for at least 12 months to qualify for the lower 12.5% LTCG rate instead of 20% STCG
  • Book ₹1.25L in LTCG every year — it's tax-free and resets your cost basis efficiently
  • Avoid debt funds if you're in a high tax bracket — post-2023, they offer no tax advantage over FDs
  • Choose growth option over dividend option — dividend taxation at slab rate erodes compounding
  • Use ELSS for 80C — it's your only mutual fund deduction and the equity growth is a bonus
  • Keep records of every SIP instalment date and NAV — critical for accurate LTCG/STCG computation on partial redemptions

Tax laws change frequently in India — always verify current rates with SEBI, the Income Tax Department website, or a qualified tax advisor before making major redemption decisions. This article reflects rules as per the Finance Act 2024.
