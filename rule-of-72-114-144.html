The Rule of 72, 114 and 144: Instant Mental Math for Every Investor

You don't always need a calculator to estimate how your money grows. Three simple rules — the Rule of 72, 114 and 144 — let you compute doubling, tripling and quadrupling times in seconds. Every serious investor should have these numbers memorised.

The Rule of 72

The Rule of 72 is a timeless mental shortcut that tells you how many years it takes for an investment to double in value at a given annual compound return. Divide 72 by the annual rate of return, and you get the approximate number of years required.

Rule of 72 Formula
Years to Double = 72 ÷ Annual Rate of Return (%)

Example: At 12% CAGR → 72 ÷ 12 = 6 years to double
Example: At 7% FD rate → 72 ÷ 7 = ~10.3 years to double

The rule works because of a mathematical approximation rooted in natural logarithms. The actual formula for doubling time is ln(2) / ln(1 + r), which equals roughly 0.693 / r. Multiplying numerator and denominator by 100 gives ~69.3/rate — and 72 is used instead because it's highly divisible (by 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, 9, 12) making mental arithmetic far easier.

Consider a ₹1 lakh investment at different return rates:

  • FD at 7%: Doubles to ₹2L in approximately 10.3 years
  • Equity mutual fund at 12%: Doubles to ₹2L in just 6 years
  • Large-cap index fund at 10%: Doubles in 7.2 years
  • Small-cap fund at 15%: Doubles in just 4.8 years
Key Takeaway: The Rule of 72 works best for annual compounding rates between 6% and 15%. Outside this range, accuracy degrades slightly — use 69.3 for lower rates and 70 for higher rates for better precision.

The Rule of 114

Once you've grasped the Rule of 72, the Rule of 114 is its natural companion. It tells you how long it takes for money to triple at a given compound rate. The logic is the same — divide 114 by your annual return rate.

Rule of 114 Formula
Years to Triple = 114 ÷ Annual Rate of Return (%)

Example: At 12% CAGR → 114 ÷ 12 = 9.5 years to triple
Example: At 7% FD rate → 114 ÷ 7 = ~16.3 years to triple

The number 114 comes from the natural log of 3 divided by a typical interest rate, scaled similarly to 72. ln(3) ≈ 1.0986, which when scaled to percentage rates gives ~109.9 — and 114 provides a slightly conservative, easy-to-divide approximation.

This rule is particularly useful for goal-planning. If your retirement goal is 3× your current savings and you're targeting 12% CAGR, you know you need roughly 9.5 years — without touching a spreadsheet. If you're at 8% returns, you'd need about 14.3 years.

The Rule of 144

The Rule of 144 completes the trilogy, estimating how long it takes to quadruple your money. Divide 144 by the annual return rate.

Rule of 144 Formula
Years to Quadruple = 144 ÷ Annual Rate of Return (%)

Example: At 12% CAGR → 144 ÷ 12 = 12 years to quadruple
Example: At 8% return → 144 ÷ 8 = 18 years to quadruple

Notice something elegant here: at 12% CAGR, your money doubles in 6 years (Rule of 72), triples in 9.5 years (Rule of 114), and quadruples in 12 years (Rule of 144). The Rule of 144 is simply two applications of the Rule of 72 — because doubling twice is quadrupling. And 72 × 2 = 144. That's the internal consistency that makes these three rules a system rather than isolated tricks.

Real-World Applications

These rules aren't just academic — they change how you think about financial decisions in real-time. Here's how to apply them to typical Indian investor scenarios:

  • Comparing FD vs equity: FD at 7% doubles in 10.3 years. Equity at 12% doubles in 6 years. Over 30 years, equity doubles 5× while FD doubles only 2.9× — a massive compounding advantage.
  • Retirement goal sanity check: Need ₹2 crore and have ₹50 lakh? That's a 4× need. At 12%, it happens in 12 years. At 8%, it takes 18 years. Planning matters.
  • Evaluating a new investment product: If someone promises your money quadruples in 5 years, that implies 144 ÷ 5 = 28.8% annual returns — a huge red flag for any legitimate regulated product.
  • Choosing between growth vs dividend options: In growth option, your NAV compounds — so Rule of 72 directly applies. In dividend option, payouts interrupt compounding, slowing your doubling time materially.

Inflation and These Rules

The most underappreciated application of these rules is understanding the enemy side — inflation. Inflation doesn't just slow growth; it actively destroys purchasing power.

Inflation Insight: If India's inflation rate is 6%, apply the Rule of 72: 72 ÷ 6 = 12 years. This means your money's purchasing power halves in just 12 years if it isn't growing at least at the inflation rate. A savings account at 3.5% post-tax is losing real value every single day.

Here's the inflation math in context:

  • At 6% inflation: Purchasing power halves in 12 years (Rule of 72)
  • At 6% inflation: Purchasing power falls to one-third in 19 years (Rule of 114)
  • At 6% inflation: Purchasing power falls to one-quarter in 24 years (Rule of 144)

Now layer your investments. If your FD earns 7% and inflation is 6%, your real return is just ~1%. At 1% real return, it takes 72 years to double in real terms. That's not wealth creation — that's barely staying above water. Equity investors targeting 12% with 6% inflation enjoy a 6% real return, meaning real doubling every 12 years.

Important: The Rule of 72, 114 and 144 are approximations — not precise calculations. They assume consistent, uninterrupted annual compounding and do not account for taxes, inflation, or variable returns. For accurate projections of your actual SIP or lumpsum investments, always use a dedicated calculator.

These three rules, taken together, give you a powerful mental model for evaluating virtually any investment opportunity. They help you spot unrealistic promises, benchmark different assets instantly, and understand inflation's compounding damage — all without a spreadsheet. Commit them to memory: 72, 114, 144. They may well be the three most important numbers in your investing life.
